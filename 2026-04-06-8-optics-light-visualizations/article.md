## 1. Prism Dispersion

When white light enters a glass prism, something remarkable happens: it doesn't just bend. It *splits*. Each wavelength refracts at a different angle — violet bends the most, red the least — and the result is a spectrum stretching from infrared to violet.

**What it does:** This [interactive prism dispersion simulator](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/prism-dispersion) lets you adjust the prism material, the angle of incidence, and watch how different wavelengths separate in real time. You can see exactly why diamond sparkles the way it does, and why prisms are still used in spectroscopy today.

**Why it matters:** Dispersion is the reason fiber optic signals degrade over distance — different wavelengths travel at slightly different speeds. Understanding this helps engineers design better communication systems.

## 2. Rainbow Formation

A rainbow isn't just "light + water." It's a precise sequence: refraction into a droplet, internal reflection, then refraction back out — with dispersion at each air-water boundary. Each color exits at a slightly different angle (roughly 40°–42° from the antisolar point), which is why rainbows are always curved.

**What it does:** The [rainbow formation visualization](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/rainbow-formation) traces individual rays through a water droplet, showing the exact angle where each color emerges. You can adjust the droplet size, light wavelength, and see why supernumerary bows (faint additional arcs below the primary rainbow) appear under specific conditions.

**Why it matters:** The same physics that creates a rainbow in your backyard powers diffraction gratings in spectroscopy labs. Understanding ray paths is the first step to understanding all wave optics.

**Try it here:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/rainbow-formation](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/rainbow-formation)

## 3. Total Internal Reflection

When light travels from a denser medium (like glass) to a less dense medium (like air), it bends *away* from the normal. Past a certain angle — the critical angle — the light doesn't refract at all. It reflects back inside the medium. This is total internal reflection, and it's the principle behind every fiber optic cable on Earth.

**What it does:** The [total internal reflection simulator](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/total-internal-reflection) shows exactly what happens at the boundary between two media. You can dial up the angle of incidence, cross the critical threshold, and watch the entire beam suddenly trap itself. It makes Snell's law viscerally intuitive.

**Why it matters:** Without total internal reflection, there'd be no fiber optic internet, no endoscopes, no prism-based binoculars. It's one of the most commercially important phenomena in optics.

**Try it here:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/total-internal-reflection](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/total-internal-reflection)

## 4. Electromagnetic Spectrum

Visible light is just one small sliver of the electromagnetic spectrum — a continuum stretching from radio waves (wavelengths the size of football fields) to gamma rays (wavelengths smaller than atomic nuclei). Visible light occupies 380–700 nanometers.

**What it does:** The [electromagnetic spectrum visualization](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/electromagnetic-spectrum) maps the full range, showing wavelength, frequency, and photon energy for each band. You can slide across the spectrum and immediately see how a Wi-Fi signal at 2.4 GHz relates to a medical X-ray at 10¹⁸ Hz — and why a microwave can cook food but visible light can't.

**Why it matters:** Understanding the spectrum is fundamental to every wireless technology — radio, TV, cell phones, GPS, satellite communications, and medical imaging all depend on different parts of it.

## 5. Tyndall Effect

Not all "clear" liquids are the same. A true solution (like salt in water) lets light pass straight through. But a colloidal solution (like milk in water) scatters light in all directions — this is the Tyndall effect, and it's how you can tell whether a liquid is a suspension or a true solution.

**What it does:** The [Tyndall effect visualization](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/tyndall-effect) simulates light passing through colloidal particles, showing how particle size and concentration affect scattering. It contrasts true solutions, colloids, and suspensions side by side, making Rayleigh scattering vs. Mie scattering visually obvious.

**Why it matters:** The Tyndall effect explains why the sky is blue (Rayleigh scattering by air molecules), why car headlights make fog glow, and why colloidal dispersions appear cloudy under side lighting. It's foundational for understanding atmospheric optics.

**Try it here:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/tyndall-effect](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/tyndall-effect)

## 6. Single Slit Diffraction

When light passes through a narrow enough aperture, it doesn't just continue straight — it spreads out in a pattern of bright and dark bands. This wave behavior is diffraction, and it sets a fundamental limit on the resolution of any optical instrument, from microscopes to telescopes.

**What it does:** The [single slit diffraction simulator](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/single-slit-diffraction) lets you adjust slit width and light wavelength, then watch the intensity pattern form on a screen. You can see exactly how narrowing the slit *widens* the central maximum — counterintuitive until you see it.

**Why it matters:** Diffraction is why you can't magnify a cell phone photo indefinitely and expect to see more detail. It's also the basis for the diffraction gratings used in spectrometers to separate light into component wavelengths with extreme precision.

**Try it here:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/single-slit-diffraction](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/single-slit-diffraction)

## 7. Polarized Light

Light is a transverse wave — it oscillates perpendicular to its direction of travel. Unpolarized light oscillates in all directions. Polarized light oscillates in only one direction. Polarizing filters block all light except that oscillating in the filter's axis.

**What it does:** The [polarized light visualization](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/polarized-light) demonstrates Malus's Law (I = I₀ cos²θ) interactively. Rotate a polarizer and watch the intensity change. You can also explore crossed polarizers (total extinction at 90°), birefringence, and why LCD screens depend entirely on polarization.

**Why it matters:** Polarization is used in photography (polarizing filters cut glare), 3D movies (alternating circular polarization for each eye), fiber optics (polarization-mode dispersion), and quantum cryptography (encoding information in photon polarization states).

**Try it here:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/polarized-light](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/polarized-light)

## 8. Optical Fiber

Modern internet runs on light through glass fibers thinner than human hair. **What it does:** You can adjust the core/cladding refractive indices, numerical aperture, and fiber length to see how each parameter affects signal transmission. Watch how pulse broadening occurs over distance, and why single-mode fibers are preferred for long-haul communication.

**Why it matters:** Fiber optic cables carry 95%+ of international internet traffic. The bandwidth of a single fiber exceeds the entire radio spectrum by orders of magnitude. Understanding the physics helps you appreciate why we're not running out of internet capacity yet.
