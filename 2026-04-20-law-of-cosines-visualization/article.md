## The Problem with Pythagoras

The Pythagorean Theorem (a² + b² = c²) is elegant. But it only works on right triangles. Step outside that constraint — an acute angle here, an obtuse angle there — and you're left staring at a triangle with no 90-degree corner, wondering where to start.

The Law of Cosines fills that gap:

**c² = a² + b² − 2ab·cos(C)**

Suddenly, you can solve any triangle. But the formula feels abstract until you see it *in action*.

## See It, Drag It, Understand It

The [Law of Cosines Visualization](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/law-of-cosines) on ElysiaTools lets you do exactly that. It's a free, browser-based tool with four modes:

### 1. Explore Mode
Drag the vertices of a triangle and watch the side lengths and angles update in real time. The formula display shows the substituted values as you go. The "Verification" panel proves the Law holds for whatever shape you create — whether it's acute, right, or obtuse.

### 2. Proof Mode
Five animated steps derive the Law of Cosines directly from the Pythagorean Theorem. Drop an altitude, apply the theorem to two right triangles, equate the expressions for the height, and out pops cos(C). It's one of those proofs that feels like magic when you see it unfold.

### 3. Examples Mode
Pre-loaded triangles for five cases: acute, right, obtuse, SAS (two sides and included angle), and SSS (three sides). Each loads instantly and demonstrates exactly when and how the formula applies.

### 4. Practice Mode
Generate random problems — find a missing side or a missing angle — and check your answers. It's a genuinely useful homework companion.

## Why This Tool Stands Out

Most geometry tools show you a static diagram. This one responds to your curiosity. You want to know what happens when the angle hits exactly 90°? Drag the vertex until C = 90° and watch cos(C) become zero — the formula collapses into the Pythagorean Theorem right before your eyes. You want to see what a negative cosine looks like? Push the angle past 90° and the third term flips sign.

The step-by-step proof mode is particularly well done. Math students know the frustration of memorizing a formula without understanding why it works. Watching the derivation unfold step by step builds the kind of intuition that survives the exam.
