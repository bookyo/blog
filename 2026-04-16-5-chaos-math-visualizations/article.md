A tiny change in initial conditions. One extra parameter adjustment. Suddenly, the predictable world vanishes.

## 1. The Double Pendulum — When Order Breaks Down

A double pendulum is two pendulums attached end-to-end. Simple, right? Except it's the canonical example of chaotic motion in classical mechanics.

![Double Pendulum](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/double-pendulum)

**What makes it special:** Even though the equations governing it are completely deterministic, its motion is fundamentally unpredictable over time. Slightly different starting positions produce wildly different trajectories.

## 2. The Lorenz Attractor — The Butterfly Effect

In 1963, meteorologist Edward Lorenz built a simplified weather model. He noticed something strange: tiny rounding differences in his simulation produced dramatically different weather outcomes. This gave us the "butch flapping butterfly's wings" metaphor.

![Lorenz Attractor](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/lorenz-attractor)

**What makes it special:** The attractor is a strange shape — not a point, not a circle, but a fractal surface that never closes and never repeats. The system traces the same attractor forever without ever crossing its own path.

**What you can do:** Drag the starting point anywhere, adjust σ (Prandtl number), ρ (Rayleigh number), and β, and watch how the system's trajectory diverges. The "tracing" mode lets you see the attractor being drawn in real-time.

🔗 [Try Lorenz Attractor](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/lorenz-attractor)

## 3. Bifurcation Diagram — How Order Becomes Chaos

The logistic map: `x_{n+1} = r · x_n · (1 - x_n)`. Four symbols. One parameter. And it produces period-doubling, chaos, and windows of order within disorder.

![Bifurcation Diagram](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/bifurcation-diagram)

**What makes it special:** As you increase `r`, the system transitions from a single stable point → 2-cycle → 4-cycle → 8-cycle → chaos. But within the chaotic region, islands of order reappear. This universal route to chaos appears in lasers, circuits, and biological populations.

**What you can do:** Zoom into any region of parameter space, toggle Feigenbaum constant markers, animate the parameter `r` to watch the transition unfold, and use density visualization to see where the system spends most of its time.

🔗 [Try Bifurcation Diagram](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/bifurcation-diagram)

## 4. Fractal Chaos Systems — Self-Similarity at Every Scale

Fractals are shapes with non-integer dimensions — they look the same at every scale. They're everywhere in nature: coastlines, mountain ranges, lightning, blood vessels.

![Fractal Chaos](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/fractal-chaos)

**What makes it special:** This visualization packs four fractal systems into one interactive tool:

- **Mandelbrot Set** — the most famous fractal, where every boundary point is infinitely complex
- **Julia Sets** — related fractals that reveal different structures depending on their parameter
- **Barnsley's IFS** — ferns and fractals generated by iterated function systems
- **Fractal Tree** — a simple branching structure that looks eerily organic
- **Box-Counting Dimension** — measure the fractal dimension of any shape

**What you can do:** Pan, zoom, click anywhere in the Mandelbrot set to see the corresponding Julia set. Adjust fractal tree parameters. Measure actual fractal dimensions. No math background required — just curiosity.

🔗 [Try Fractal Chaos Systems](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/fractal-chaos)

## 5. Brownian Motion & Random Walk — When Randomness Wins

Einstein's 1905 paper explained how pollen grains jiggle in water — not from some mystical life force, but from molecules bombarding them from all sides. This random walk is the foundation of quantitative finance, signal processing, and statistical physics.

![Brownian Motion](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/brownian-motion-random-walk)

**What makes it special:** The same math that describes particle diffusion in a fluid also describes stock price movements (geometric Brownian motion), thermal noise in circuits, and search algorithms. Understanding random walks is understanding a chunk of the modern world.

**What you can do:** Switch between three modes — **Physics** (Einstein's diffusion), **Finance** (geometric Brownian motion with drift and volatility), and **Math** (pure random walk). Overlay multiple particles. Watch the distribution converge (or diverge) over time.

🔗 [Try Brownian Motion & Random Walk](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/brownian-motion-random-walk)
