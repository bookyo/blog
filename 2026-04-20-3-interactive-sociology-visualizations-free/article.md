## 1. Schelling Segregation Model

Thomas Schelling won a Nobel Prize for a model that sounds almost too simple: what if people only prefer to live near *some* neighbors like themselves — not all of them?

The [Schelling Segregation Model](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/schelling-segregation) on ElysiaTools lets you adjust the "similarity threshold" — the percentage of same-group neighbors each person wants — and watch what happens to the population.

**What you'll see:** With a threshold of just 30%, the board doesn't look random. It looks *segregated*. Agents start moving. Clusters form. By the time it stabilizes, you have clear racial or group boundaries — even though nobody *wanted* segregation.

**Why it matters:** This means you don't need bigots to get segregation. You just need mild preferences and a functioning housing market. In other words, the system does the dirty work.

**What you can do:** Adjust the density, the threshold, and the grid size. Watch how long it takes to stabilize. Try to find the "tipping point" where mild preference becomes full separation.

## 2. Motherhood Penalty & Fatherhood Premium

The gender wage gap isn't just about pay for equal work. A huge part of it is what happens *after* people become parents.

The [Motherhood Penalty & Fatherhood Premium](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/motherhood-penalty) visualization lets you simulate how parenthood affects career trajectories differently for men and women — across different countries, industries, and life stages.

**What you'll see:** The "motherhood penalty" shows up as slower wage growth, reduced promotion rates, and higher career exit probability after childbirth. Fathers, by contrast, often see a *premium* — colleagues and managers assume they're more stable, responsible, committed.

**Why it matters:** These aren't hypotheticals. Studies across the US, UK, Germany, and Scandinavia show the same pattern. The visualization lets you compare how these effects vary internationally — and how policy (paid parental leave, childcare) shapes the gap.

**What you can do:** Use the career simulator to model different scenarios. Compare the lifetime earnings impact of a child across different family structures. See how the gap evolves over a 20-year career arc.

## 3. Structural Holes — Social Network Brokerage

Not all social connections are equal. Ronald Burt's "structural holes" theory argues that your *position* in a network matters more than how many people you know.

The [Structural Holes visualization](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/structural-holes) on ElysiaTools lets you build and manipulate social networks and see exactly how brokerage works.

**What you'll see:** A "hole" is a gap between two groups that don't talk to each other. If you're the only person connecting both groups, you're a *broker* — and that unique position gives you access to non-redundant information and control over what flows between clusters.

**Why it matters:** In business, brokerage is why consultants, bankers, and dealmakers command premium fees. They see what Group A doesn't know Group B needs, and vice versa. Burt's research found that managers who spanned structural holes were promoted faster and earned more — controlling for everything else.

**What you can do:** Place nodes in different clusters and watch how the network topology changes when you add or remove bridges. See how information travels through redundant ties vs. structural holes. Find the "brokerage bottleneck" in a network.

## The Pattern Across All Three

Schelling, the motherhood penalty, and structural holes are different phenomena — but they share a core insight:

**The system produces outcomes that nobody consciously chose.**

- Mild individual preferences → systemic segregation
- Parental status → gender wage gaps that persist for decades
- Network position → career outcomes that have nothing to do with talent

These visualizations don't just teach you the theories. They let you *feel* the mechanism — which is why they're so much more memorable than a textbook paragraph.

## What Nobody's Solved Yet

Here's the question these visualizations raise that nobody has answered well:

**How do you design institutions that counteract these emergent biases — without micromanaging every individual decision?**

We know the motherhood penalty exists. We know mild preferences produce segregation. We know structural holes create winners and losers in networks. But the policy levers are blunt, and the second-order effects are hard to predict.

What would a housing policy that actually works look like — not just in theory but in simulation? Can HR policies actually close the motherhood gap, or do they just shift it? And as AI reshapes professional networks, does the value of structural holes go up or down?

These questions are wide open. The visualizations help you ask better ones.
