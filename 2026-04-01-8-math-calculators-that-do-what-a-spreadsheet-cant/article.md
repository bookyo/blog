## 3. Standard Deviation Calculator — See the Shape of Your Data

Standard deviation tells you how clustered or spread out a dataset is. A dataset with a mean of 50 and a standard deviation of 5 looks completely different from one with a standard deviation of 20 — even with the same average.

The [Standard Deviation Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/standard-deviation-calculator) handles both sample (n-1) and population (n) calculations. Paste in a comma-separated list of numbers. Get back the standard deviation, variance, mean, coefficient of variation, and one-sigma, two-sigma, and three-sigma ranges.

**What sigma intervals tell you:** In a normal distribution, roughly 68% of values fall within one sigma of the mean, 95% within two sigma, and 99.7% within three. This is how analysts spot outliers — without eyeballing a chart.

Use it when: analyzing experiment results, evaluating test score distributions, or assessing volatility in financial data.

## 4. Z-Score Calculator — Where Does This Value Actually Sit?

A z-score tells you how many standard deviations a value sits from the mean of its dataset. A z-score of 2.3 means the value is 2.3 standard deviations above the average — roughly the top 1%.

The [Z-Score Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/z-score-calculator) computes this two ways: from a provided dataset (it derives mean and standard deviation automatically) or from manually entered parameters. It also estimates the percentile — a z-score of 1.96 returns approximately the 97.5th percentile, which is the exact boundary of a 95% confidence interval.

This is the tool behind grading on a curve, comparing scores across different tests, or interpreting where a measurement falls in a normal distribution.

Use it when: grading on a curve, comparing standardized assessment results, or interpreting any normally distributed measurement.

## 5. Correlation Calculator — Are These Two Things Actually Related?

Correlation measures the linear relationship between two variables on a scale from -1 to +1. A correlation of +0.9 means when one variable goes up, the other almost always goes up too. A correlation of -0.8 means they move in opposite directions. Zero means no linear relationship.

The [Correlation Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/correlation-calculator) accepts data as paired rows (1, 52 / 2, 57 / 3, 63...) or as two separate X and Y lists. It returns Pearson's r, r-squared, covariance, and a plain-English interpretation: "weak positive correlation," "very strong negative correlation," and so on.

**What r-squared means:** If r = 0.8, r-squared = 0.64. Sixty-four percent of the variation in Y is explained by X. The other 36% comes from other factors.

Use it when: testing whether study time predicts test scores, whether marketing spend correlates with revenue, or whether two biological measurements are linked.

## 6. Confidence Interval Calculator — The Answer Is a Range

No statistical estimate is a single number. The real answer is a range, and how wide that range is tells you how confident you can be.

The [Confidence Interval Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/confidence-interval-calculator) handles two types: mean intervals (for continuous data like height, temperature, or response times) and proportion intervals (for percentages, conversion rates, or pass/fail counts).

For means, paste raw data and let it compute the standard deviation automatically, or enter summary statistics directly. For proportions, input successes and total trials. Choose confidence levels from 80% to 99% — most research uses 95%, which corresponds to a z-critical value of 1.96.

**What the interval width tells you:** A 95% confidence interval of [48.7, 52.3] means the true mean likely falls in that range. A wider interval means more uncertainty — which often means you need more data.

Use it when: reporting survey margins, interpreting election polls, or calculating error ranges for scientific measurements.

## 7. Sample Size Calculator — Don't Launch That Survey Yet

Survey 50 people when you needed 500. Your results will be too noisy to be useful. Survey 5,000 when 400 would suffice. You've wasted time and money.

The [Sample Size Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/sample-size-calculator) tells you exactly how many responses you need before you start collecting data.

It works in two modes: proportion mode (for conversion rates, pass rates, yes/no measurements) and mean mode (for continuous measurements like income or response time). Set your desired confidence level and margin of error, and the tool applies the appropriate z-critical value formula.

It also handles finite population correction. If you're surveying employees at a company of 300 people, you don't need the same sample size as for an infinite population.

Use it when: planning user research, designing A/B tests, or writing a research proposal that requires a power analysis.
