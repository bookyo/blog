## 2. Pascal's Law — The Physics Behind Hydraulic Lifts

Pascal's Law states that pressure applied to an enclosed fluid transmits equally in all directions. This is how car brakes, hydraulic jacks, and hospital lifts work — and it's counterintuitive until you see it.

The interactive hydraulic press simulation lets you apply force on one piston and watch the force multiply on the other, based on the area ratio. Adjust piston sizes, add loads, and watch pressure transmission in real time.

**Use it when:** You're engineering mechanical systems, studying fluid dynamics, or need to explain hydraulics to someone who just doesn't get it.

## 3. Coriolis Force — Why Toilets Swirl Differently in Each Hemisphere

The Coriolis effect deflects moving objects due to Earth's rotation. It's responsible for hurricane spin directions, the drift of artillery shells, and yes — the direction water swirls in your toilet (though that's more complicated than people think).

This visualization shows the Coriolis effect on a rotating Earth. You can launch projectiles, watch the Foucault pendulum swing, and see how atmospheric and oceanic circulations form. Adjust the rotation speed and latitude to see how deflection changes.

**Use it when:** You want to finally settle the toilet-swirl debate, study meteorology, or understand why satellite imagery shows hurricanes spinning clockwise in the south.

👉 [Try Coriolis Force](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/coriolis-force)

## 4. Gravitational Waves — Listening to the Universe

In 2015, LIGO detected gravitational waves from two black holes merging 1.3 billion light-years away. The signal lasted 0.2 seconds and stretched a 4km laser arm by less than 1/1000th of a proton's width.

This visualization simulates gravitational waves from binary black hole mergers. Watch spacetime distort as two orbiting black holes spiral inward and merge. The visualization includes a simplified LIGO detector simulation showing how the wave signal appears in the laser interferometer data.

**Use it when:** You're teaching general relativity, want to understand LIGO's Nobel Prize-winning discovery, or need to show students why gravitational waves matter.

## 5. Moon Phases — Why the Moon Looks Different Every Night

The lunar cycle takes about 29.5 days, and the moon's appearance changes based on the relative positions of the Sun, Earth, and Moon. Most people know the names (full moon, new moon, crescent) but don't understand *why* the phases happen.

This interactive visualization shows the Sun-Earth-Moon system from a top-down view. Move through time to see how the shadow angle changes, and watch the corresponding moon appearance update in real time. It also shows why solar eclipses are rare (the Moon's orbit is tilted 5° relative to Earth's).

**Use it when:** You're planning astrophotography, teaching astronomy, or want to explain to kids why the moon looks like a different shape every night.

## 6. Universe Expansion — Hubble's Law in Action

The universe is expanding. Edwin Hubble discovered in 1929 that galaxies farther away from us recede faster — the farther you look, the faster they move. This relationship is now known as the Hubble law.

This visualization lets you explore universe expansion through Hubble's law. Watch the scale factor evolve over cosmic time, see how redshift relates to distance, and explore the relationship between cosmic evolution and the observable universe's growth.

**Use it when:** You're studying cosmology, want to understand dark energy, or are trying to wrap your head around why the universe has no center.

## 7. Huygens' Principle — How Waves Actually Propagate

Every point on a wavefront acts as a source of secondary spherical wavelets. The new wavefront is the envelope of all these wavelets. This principle explains diffraction, refraction, and interference — the core behaviors of all wave phenomena.

The interactive Huygens' principle visualization lets you draw wavefronts, watch secondary wavelets emanate from each point, and see the new wavefront emerge as their envelope. It's the clearest way to understand why waves bend around corners and focus through apertures.

**Use it when:** You're studying optics, wave mechanics, or need a visual proof for Huygens' construction that students can actually manipulate.

## 8. Surface Tension — Why Water Forms Droplets

Surface tension is the cohesive force that makes water droplets spherical, allows insects to walk on water, and powers capillary action in plants. It's caused by intermolecular forces pulling liquid molecules inward, minimizing surface area.

This visualization shows surface tension effects: water striders walking on a pond, droplets forming and merging, capillary rise in thin tubes, and the balance between cohesive and adhesive forces. Adjust parameters like liquid density, temperature, and contact angle to see how surface tension changes.

**Use it when:** You're studying fluid mechanics, need to explain capillary action in biology class, or want to understand why small insects can seemingly defy gravity on water surfaces.
