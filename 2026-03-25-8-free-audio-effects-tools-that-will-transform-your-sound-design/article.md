## 1. Audio Bitcrusher — Lo-Fi Digital Distortion

Remember the crunchy sound of early video game consoles and samplers? That's bitcrushing—the deliberate degradation of audio quality by reducing sample rate and bit depth.

The [Audio Bitcrusher tool](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/audio-bitcrusher) gives you four intensity presets: Mild Lo-Fi for subtle texture, Classic Bitcrush for that authentic 90s sampler sound, and Heavy Digital Distortion for something genuinely abrasive.

Beyond presets, you can fine-tune sample rate (1,000–48,000 Hz), bit depth (1–16 bits), dithering type, and wet/dry mix. The dithering options—rectangular, triangular, and highpass—matter more than they sound. Add too little dither and quantization noise becomes harsh; the right dither type spreads that noise into something less offensive to the ear.

This means you can emulate the exact character of a 4-bit Akai sampler, an 8-bit Nintendo, or a 12-bit Atari ST—all without hunting down vintage gear.

**Use it for:** retro game soundtracks, lo-fi hip-hop, sound design for glitch art, adding grit to sterile digital recordings.

## 2. Audio Vibrato — Pitch Modulation

Vibrato is a periodic pitch oscillation—think of the wavering sustain of a violin or the subtle pitch bends in a synth lead. It's one of the most fundamental expressive techniques in music.

The [Audio Vibrato tool](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/audio-vibrato) keeps it focused: rate (0.5–20 Hz) and depth (0–1) are your two controls. That's it. No menu diving, no hidden sub-modes.

Rate at 5 Hz with depth at 0.5 gives you a classic musical vibrato. Crank the rate to 10+ Hz with depth near 1 and you've got a kosmic wobble bass effect. Lower both and you get subtle, almost subliminal pitch movement.

The output supports seven formats including FLAC and WAV for lossless quality, and MP3, AAC, M4A, OGG, and Opus for web use.

**Use it for:** guitar solos, synth leads, vocal ornamentation, sound effects, electronic music textures.

## 3. Audio Tremolo — Amplitude Modulation

Don't confuse tremolo with vibrato. Tremolo modulates volume—rhythmic pumping, fading in and out. It's what gives surf guitar its signature chop, and what creates that pulsing intensity in horror film scores.

The [Audio Tremolo tool](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/audio-tremolo) offers the same clean interface: rate (0.5–20 Hz) and depth (0–1) controls. Depth at 0.7 is the sweet spot for that classic amp tremolo sound—present but not overwhelming.

At extreme settings, tremolo becomes a creative effect in its own right. A fast rate (15+ Hz) with full depth creates a Ring Modulator-adjacent metallic texture. A slow rate (0.5–1 Hz) with high depth makes the audio breathe.

**Use it for:** surf rock guitar, horror soundtracks, rhythmic texture, adding movement to static pad sounds.

## 4. Audio Chorus — Sound Thickening

Chorus works by layering multiple slightly delayed and pitch-modulated copies of the original signal. The result: a single voice sounds like two, three, or more playing together.

The [Audio Chorus tool](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/audio-chorus) goes deeper than most chorus plugins. You control individual "voices"—each with its own input gain, output gain, delay (5–100 ms), decay, speed, and depth. The default preset uses two voices, but you can add more for an even thicker sound.

This is the secret weapon for thin recordings. A direct guitar DI with chorus applied can sound like a mic'd amp in a room. A solo vocal with chorus sounds like a group.

**Use it for:** thickening thin recordings, double-tracking without recording twice, converting mono to pseudo-stereo, making direct guitar DIs mixable.

## 5. Audio Flanger — Sweeping Comb Filter

Flanging creates that distinctive "whooshing" sweep by mixing the original signal with a delayed, modulated copy. The delay is typically very short (under 1 ms) and the modulation creates the sweeping comb-filter effect.

The [Audio Flanger tool](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/audio-flanger) gives you surgical control: base delay (0–30 ms), depth (0–30 ms), regeneration/feedback (-95 to +95), width, speed, phase (0–180°), and interpolation type.

High regeneration creates resonant, Jet-like sweeps. Negative regeneration (cancellation) thins the sound. The phase control affects how the left and right channels sweep relative to each other—25° is a common starting point for stereo material.

**Use it for:** classic rock guitar solos, psychedelic textures, synth pads, electronic swooshes, anything that needs a sense of motion.

## 6. Audio Phaser — Phase-Shifted Sweeps

While flanging uses delay, the [Audio Phaser](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/audio-phaser) uses phase cancellation via all-pass filters. The result is similar in character—a sweeping, liquid texture—but with a different underlying mechanism and a distinctly spacey quality.

Controls include input/output gain, delay (0.1–10 ms), decay, speed, and phase type (triangle or sine wave). Triangle waves create sharper, more aggressive sweeps; sine waves are smoother and more liquid.

The 0.4 input gain and 0.74 output gain defaults are carefully chosen to work across a wide range of audio without clipping or excessive attenuation.

**Use it for:** electric piano, synth leads, space rock, adding sci-fi character to sound effects, anything that needs "phased" rather than "flanged."

## 7. Audio De-Esser — Sibilance Control

Here's one that's less about creative effect and more about problem-solving: de-essing targets harsh "s" sounds in vocal recordings. Run a track through a de-esser and those sharp, fatiguing sibilants smooth out without dulling the overall vocal.

The [Audio De-Esser](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/audio-de-esser) uses dynamic equalization rather than a simple band-split compressor. You set a center frequency (2,000–12,000 Hz), bandwidth, threshold, and ratio. The default 6,500 Hz with 4,000 Hz bandwidth targets the most common sibilant range.

The threshold and ratio controls are key. A low threshold with a high ratio clamps down hard on any sibilance that crosses the threshold. A higher threshold with a moderate ratio catches only the harshest peaks.

**Use it for:** podcast vocals, audiobooks, broadcast voiceovers, any spoken-word recording where "s" sounds are too aggressive.

## 8. Audio Room Simulator — Acoustic Environment

The [Audio Room Simulator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/audio-room-simulator) is the most sophisticated tool in this set. It simulates the acoustic characteristics of real rooms—from a small closet to a concert hall—by controlling early reflections, reverb decay, high-frequency damping, and more.

Four room presets get you in the ballpark instantly: Closet (1–2m), Bedroom (3–4m), Living Room (5–6m), and Concert Hall (10m+). Custom mode lets you dial in room size, decay time (0.1–10 seconds), damping, and early reflection strength independently.

This means you can record a clean studio vocal and place it in a bathroom, a gymnasium, or a cathedral—all without leaving your chair. The wet/dry balance adjusts automatically based on room size.

**Use it for:** placing recordings in acoustic contexts, adding depth to sterile recordings, sound design for film/video, simulating live recording environments for demos.
