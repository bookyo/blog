Whether you're a runner chasing a sub-3 hour marathon, a basketball player trying to get noticed by college scouts, or a weekend warrior who just wants to know if their tennis serve is actually improving—there's a calculator for that. And not the basic ones on your phone. I'm talking about tools that give you professional-grade analysis for free.

## 1. Marathon Time Predictor

**What it does:** Plug in a recent race result—say, a half marathon time—and this tool runs three scientifically validated formulas (Riegel, Cameron, and Purdue) to predict your marathon finish time. It spits out split predictions, recommended pace per kilometer and mile, and training tips tailored to your goal.

**Why it matters:** Most runners overestimate or underestimate their marathon potential by a shocking margin. The difference between a 3:30 and a 3:45 finish isn't just 15 minutes—it's a completely different nutrition strategy, pacing plan, and training block. This tool uses real exercise physiology formulas, not guesswork.

**Use it when:** You're 12–16 weeks out from race day and building your training plan around a realistic target.

**Try it:** [Marathon Time Predictor](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/marathon-time)

## 2. Running Pace Calculator

**What it does:** Enter any distance and time, and get your pace per kilometer, per mile, speed in km/h and mph, plus projected finish times for 5K, 10K, half marathon, and full marathon. It works in both directions—enter a target pace and see what your 10K finish time would look like.

**Why it matters:** Most runners know their pace for one distance but have no idea what it means for another. A 5:30/km pace doesn't automatically translate to a sub-50 minute 10K if you don't account for pacing strategy and fatigue. This tool does that math for you instantly.

**Use it when:** You're race week and deciding whether to go out at 5:20/km or 5:25/km—and want to know the downstream impact on every distance you care about.

**Try it:** [Running Pace Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/running-pace)

## 3. Basketball Stats Calculator

**What it does:** Input your season totals—points, rebounds, assists, steals, blocks, turnovers, field goals, free throws, three-pointers—and get per-game averages, shooting percentages (FG%, 3P%, FT%), advanced metrics like True Shooting Percentage (TS%) and Player Efficiency Rating (PER), plus an overall rating from "Developing" to "Superstar."

**Why it matters:** College coaches and scouts don't just look at points. They look at efficiency—who can score without hogging shots, who contributes when not scoring. This calculator breaks down the numbers coaches actually care about, giving you the language to talk about your game intelligently.

**Use it when:** You want to understand your stat sheet the way a recruiter does, or you're comparing two players on your team for lineup decisions.

**Try it:** [Basketball Stats Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/basketball-stats)

## 4. Football Stats Calculator

**What it does:** Enter goals, shots, shots on target, assists, games played, and minutes—and get goals per game, goals per 90 minutes, shooting percentage, goal conversion rate, assists per game, and ratings for overall performance, scoring, efficiency, and playmaking.

**Why it matters:** A striker who scores 10 goals from 80 shots is very different from one who scores 10 from 25. This tool reveals the difference between clinical finishers and volume shooters—exactly what coaches and scouts look for when evaluating forwards.

**Use it when:** You're reviewing your own season data, scouting opponents, or building a player comparison report.

**Try it:** [Football Stats Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/football-stats)

## 5. One Rep Max Calculator

**What it does:** Enter the weight and reps you lifted for any exercise—say, 100kg for 5 reps—and choose between the Epley, Brzycki, or Lander formula. It estimates your theoretical one-rep maximum and then calculates your training percentages from 50% to 95%, giving you a complete periodization chart in seconds.

**Why it matters:** Programming your training with percentages of your actual 1RM is the difference between a generic program and one calibrated to your current strength level. Most people guess at their 1RM. This tool calculates it from real data you already have.

**Use it when:** You're writing a strength program and need to convert "I benched 100kg for 5" into "my 1RM is approximately 115kg, so 85% for sets of 5 is 97kg."

**Try it:** [One Rep Max Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/one-rep-max)

## 6. Triathlon Calculator

**What it does:** Enter your swim, bike, and run split times along with transition times (T1 and T2), and get your total race time, pace per 100m for swimming, bike speed in km/h, run pace per km, a breakdown of each split, and the percentage of total time spent in each discipline and transitions.

**Why it matters:** Triathlon is won and lost in transitions and pacing balance. Most age-groupers swim too hard, blow up on the bike, and then limp through the run. Knowing exactly what percentage of your total time goes to each leg reveals where to focus your training energy.

**Use it when:** You're analyzing a completed race or building a race-day pacing strategy for an upcoming event.

**Try it:** [Triathlon Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/triathlon-calculator)

## 7. Tennis Serve Speed Calculator

**What it does:** Enter the distance from baseline to net (23.77m by default—the official court dimension) and the time it takes for the ball to travel that distance. It calculates speed in m/s, km/h, and mph, then classifies your serve against standard benchmarks: Learning Stage, Beginner, Intermediate, Advanced, or Professional Level. It also gives a comparison to ATP serve speeds.

**Why it matters:** Serve speed is one of the most measurable, improvable aspects of tennis. Knowing whether your 140 km/h serve puts you in "recreational" or "competitive" territory helps you set concrete development goals. And it's oddly motivating to track.

**Use it when:** You're a coach tracking player development over a season, or a recreational player wanting objective benchmarks for improvement.

**Try it:** [Tennis Serve Speed Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/tennis-serve-speed)

**Why it matters:** Pool swimming is deceptively hard to track. Unlike running, where GPS watches give you instant pace feedback, swimmers often train by feel. This tool gives you concrete numbers—exactly what you need to structure interval training with precise rest periods.

**Use it when:** You're programming swim intervals and need to convert "I swam 1500m in 25:00" into "my 100m pace is 1:40, so 10×100 on 1:50 gives me 10 seconds rest."

## The Problem Nobody Is Solving

Here's what all 8 of these tools share: they take data athletes already have and extract signal from it. No expensive sensors. No subscriptions. No learning curve.

But there's a gap they don't yet fill: **cross-sport comparison.** A marathon runner, a basketball player, and a powerlifter have completely different ways of measuring progress—and there's no single framework that lets you compare improvement across disciplines in a meaningful way.

Some platforms are trying. But right now, if you're a triathlete who also lifts and wants to track whether your swim improvement is outpacing your strength gains, you're stitching together spreadsheets.

That's the next tool that needs to exist. And based on the quality of what's already in this collection, I have a feeling someone will build it soon.
