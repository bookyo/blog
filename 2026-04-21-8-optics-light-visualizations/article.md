# 8 Free Optics & Light Visualizations That Make Physics Actually Click

Light is everywhere — yet most of us couldn't explain how a rainbow forms, why the sky is blue, or how fiber optics transmit data at the speed of light. These 8 interactive visualizations change that. All free, all in your browser, no sign-up required.

## 1. [Prism Dispersion](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/prism-dispersion)

White light enters a glass prism and exits as a rainbow. This tool simulates exactly that — watch how different wavelengths refract at different angles, producing the classic ROYGBIV spectrum. Adjust the prism angle, toggle between preset scenarios (rainbow colors, minimum deviation, high dispersion), and see the spectrum bar update in real time.

**Why it matters:** Dispersion is why diamonds sparkle and why you see halos around streetlights. Once you see it interactively, the math (Snell's law, Cauchy equation) finally makes sense.

## 2. [Rainbow Formation](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/rainbow-formation)

A rainbow isn't just "light bending." It's two refractions + one internal reflection inside millions of water droplets, with dispersion splitting colors at each step. This visualization shows the droplet cross-section with real-time ray tracing — incident angle, refraction angle, deviation angle — plus the observed rainbow arc. The primary rainbow appears at 42° for red light and 40° for violet.

**This means:** The dark band between primary and secondary rainbows (Alexander's dark band) exists because light at those angles has already been reflected outside the rainbow-forming range.

## 3. [Total Internal Reflection](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/total-internal-reflection)

When light travels from a denser medium (glass, water) to a less dense medium (air), something strange happens beyond the critical angle (~41.8° for glass-air): all the light reflects back instead of escaping. No absorption, no refraction — 100% reflection. This tool shows the ray diagram, the critical angle indicator, and even an optical fiber demo that puts TIR to practical use.

**In other words:** Total internal reflection is the reason your fiber internet works. Light bounces down a glass fiber at just the right angle, losing virtually no energy over kilometers.

## 4. [Electromagnetic Spectrum](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/electromagnetic-spectrum)

From 10⁶ Hz radio waves to 10²⁰ Hz gamma rays — the EM spectrum spans 14 orders of magnitude. This visualization maps wavelength, frequency, and photon energy on a single interactive canvas. Hover over any band (radio, microwave, infrared, visible, UV, X-ray, gamma) to see real-world examples and the physics behind each region.

**Key insight:** Visible light is just a thin slice (380–700 nm) of a massive spectrum. Your WiFi, microwave, and medical imaging all use the same electromagnetic phenomenon — just at different frequencies.

## 5. [Tyndall Effect](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/tyndall-effect)

Shine a beam of light through a colloidal solution and the path becomes visible — scattering off particles smaller than the wavelength of visible light. This visualization demonstrates Rayleigh scattering intensity (∝ 1/λ⁴), comparing how blue light scatters ~5× more than red. Switch between light beam mode, particle scattering, solution comparison (true solution vs. colloid vs. suspension), and wavelength spectrum analysis.

**Why the sky is blue:** Shorter wavelengths scatter more. Blue light (~450 nm) scatters roughly 5.6× more than red light (~650 nm), making the daytime sky appear blue despite the sun emitting white light.

## 6. [Single Slit Diffraction](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/single-slit-diffraction)

Light passing through a narrow slit doesn't cast a sharp shadow — it creates a central bright band flanked by alternating dark and bright fringes. This tool visualizes the optical path diagram, real-time intensity distribution I(θ) = I₀·[sin(β)/β]², and the diffraction pattern. Adjust slit width (a) and wavelength (λ) to see how the central fringe width changes.

**This means:** The resolution limit of microscopes and telescopes isn't just about lens quality — it's a fundamental diffraction limit. You physically cannot resolve two points closer together than about λ/2.

## 7. [Polarized Light](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/polarized-light)

Unpolarized light becomes polarized after passing through a polarizer. Malus's law (I = I₀ cos²θ) governs the intensity after the second polarizer — if the axes are perpendicular (90°), no light gets through. This visualization shows unpolarized light becoming linearly polarized, then demonstrates rotation, wave plates (quarter-wave, half-wave), and circular/elliptical polarization states.

**Real-world application:** LCD screens, polarized sunglasses, 3D movie glasses, and fiber optic communications all rely on polarization control. Polarizers block glare by eliminating reflections oriented horizontally — which is why flipping your sunglasses 90° doesn't fix the glare.

---

## 8. [Optical Fiber](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/optical-fiber)

How does light actually travel through a fiber optic cable? This tool simulates a fiber cross-section, the light propagation path via total internal reflection, numerical aperture (NA), acceptance angle, and critical angle. Watch how light rays enter at different angles — only those within the acceptance cone (determined by NA) will propagate via TIR.

**The number that matters:** A typical silica fiber has NA ≈ 0.12–0.20, giving an acceptance angle of ~7–11.5°. Bend the fiber too sharply and rays exceed the critical angle — causing signal loss.
