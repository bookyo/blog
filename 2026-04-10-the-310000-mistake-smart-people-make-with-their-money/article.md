## Rule of 72 Calculator

**The one mental math trick finance pros actually use**

Here's a number that will stick with you: **72**. Divide it by your annual interest rate, and you get roughly how many years it takes your money to double.

At 6% return: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years.
At 8% return: 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years.
At 12% return: 72 ÷ 12 = 6 years.

This is the Rule of 72 — a shortcut so elegant that it has survived centuries of financial innovation.

But here's what most people don't know: the Rule of 72 is just the start. The [Rule of 72 Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/rule-of-72) goes further. It compares all three rules (72, 69, and 70), shows you the exact error margins, and tells you which rule is most accurate for your specific rate.

At 4% interest, the Rule of 69 outperforms Rule of 72. At 8%, Rule of 72 is your best bet. The calculator doesn't just give you a number — it shows you the comparison table so you understand *why*.

**Why it matters:** The Rule of 72 is the foundation of financial intuition. Once you can double your money mentally, you start asking better questions: Is my 4% savings account actually working? What's a realistic doubling timeline for my retirement fund?

The [Amortization Schedule](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/amortization-schedule) tool generates a complete payment breakdown in seconds. Enter your loan amount, interest rate, and term, and you get:

- Year-by-year breakdown of principal vs interest

Here's a number that surprises people: On a $300,000, 30-year mortgage at 6.5%, you pay more in interest than principal. Your $1,896 monthly payment feels manageable until you realize you're handing the bank $383,000 in interest over three decades.

**Why it matters:** Amortization is how banks make money. Understanding it is how you decide whether to refinance, make extra payments, or pay off a loan early. The calculator shows you exactly when the tipping point happens — when your payments start going more to principal than interest (hint: it's not halfway through your loan).

## Debt Snowball Calculator

**The psychological advantage that makes debt payoff 3x faster**

The debt snowball method is simple: pay off your smallest balance first, then roll that payment into the next smallest. It sounds counterintuitive — shouldn't you tackle high-interest debt first? Here's the psychology: quick wins build momentum. Paying off a $500 credit card in 3 months feels like a victory. That victory keeps you motivated to attack the next one.

The [Debt Snowball Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/debt-snowball-calculator) handles up to 5 debts and shows you:

- Payoff order (smallest balance first)
- Total interest paid
- Payoff date for each debt
- Total months to debt freedom

It also shows the psychological milestone: when each debt gets paid off. That visibility matters more than you'd think. Most people quit debt payoff plans because they can't see progress. The snowball makes progress visible.

**Why it matters:** Debt payoff is 80% psychology, 20% math. The snowball method works because it engineers early victories that sustain motivation. This calculator gives you both the math and the momentum.

## Daily Compound Interest Calculator

**Where the math gets satisfying**

Compound interest is called the eighth wonder of the world for a reason. Einstein allegedly called it the most powerful force in the universe. What's less known: compounding frequency matters more than most people realize.

$10,000 at 8% annual interest:
- Compounded annually: $10,800 after 1 year
- Compounded monthly: $10,830 after 1 year
- Compounded daily: $10,833 after 1 year

The difference seems small on $10,000. Scale it to $500,000 over 30 years, and daily compounding vs annual compounding is the difference between $5,340,000 and $5,030,000. That's a $310,000 gap from one setting.

## Capital Gains Tax Calculator

**The tax question that trips up every investor**

You sold a stock for a profit. How much do you actually owe? The answer depends on holding period (short-term vs long-term), your income bracket, and whether you're in a state that taxes capital gains. The [Capital Gains Tax Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/capital-gains-tax) handles all three.

It calculates:
- Short-term capital gains (held less than 1 year, taxed as ordinary income)
- Long-term capital gains (held over 1 year, lower tax rates)
- Net investment income tax (3.8% for high earners)
- After-tax proceeds

The hidden insight: short-term vs long-term classification changes everything. A $50,000 gain in the 24% bracket costs you $12,000 in taxes short-term, but only $10,000 long-term. That's $2,000 saved by waiting 365 days. Before you sell, you need to know which rate applies.

**Why it matters:** Capital gains taxes are the most commonly miscalculated tax in personal finance. Most people know "long-term is better" but don't realize how much the difference is — or that state taxes can add 5-13% on top of federal rates. This calculator shows you the full picture.
