## What a Solstice Actually Is

The shorthand version: summer solstice is the longest day, winter solstice is the shortest. Equinoxes are the days with equal day and night. That's accurate enough for most conversations, but it misses the interesting parts.

A solstice is an *instant* — a specific moment when Earth's axial tilt reaches its maximum inclination toward or away from the Sun. It isn't a day, it's a moment. The June solstice typically occurs on June 20–22. The December solstice usually lands on December 21–22. But those dates are expressed in UTC. Depending on your time zone, the moment might fall on a different calendar day.

An equinox is the moment the Sun crosses the celestial equator — the projection of Earth's equator into space. On those two days, nearly every place on Earth receives approximately 12 hours of daylight and 12 hours of darkness. The word "equinox" comes from the Latin *aequus* (equal) and *nox* (night).

The complexity accumulates quickly. Earth's orbit is elliptical, which means we're closer to the Sun in January than in July. This, combined with the axial precession (a 26,000-year wobble in Earth's orientation), causes the solstice and equinox dates to drift slightly year to year. Most people don't notice or care. But for anyone tracking seasonal patterns — farmers, photographers, event planners, people with seasonal mood shifts — that drift has real consequences.
