## 3. Daily Compound Interest Calculator

Compound interest is called the eighth wonder of the world for a reason. But most calculators compound monthly or annually. This one compounds daily — and the difference is real.

Start with $10,000 at 5% annual interest, add $500 a month, and let it run for 20 years. With daily compounding, you end up with more than you would with monthly compounding. Stretch it to 30 years and the difference compounds into thousands of dollars.

The tool outputs the final amount, total principal, total interest earned, APY, and a year-by-year breakdown so you can watch your balance compound in real time. It also supports choosing when during the month your contribution lands — beginning or end — which slightly affects the end result.

This means you can model actual savings scenarios. What if you increase your monthly contribution by $200 after a raise? What if you start five years earlier? Run both. Compare. Adjust. That's how financial planning actually works — not by guessing, but by modeling and measuring.

**Use it when:** you're projecting savings goals — retirement, a house down payment, a child's education — and want the most accurate compound interest projection available for free.

## 5. Annuity Calculator

An annuity is essentially the inverse of a loan. Instead of borrowing money and paying it back, you're making regular payments into an account that grows with interest — and then, at some point, the account pays you back.

This calculator handles three types of annuity math: the future value of a series of payments (how much your contributions will grow to), the present value of a series of payments (what a future income stream is worth today), and the payment amount needed to reach a target future or present value.

The practical use case is retirement planning. If you know you want $60,000 a year in retirement income for 20 years, this calculator works backward to tell you how much you need to have saved when you retire — and how much you need to be contributing now to get there.

**Use it when:** you're thinking about retirement income, pension decisions, or structured investment products and need to understand the math behind them.

## 6. Capital Gains Tax Calculator

Most investment return calculators stop at the gain. This one goes one step further: it tells you how much of that gain you'll actually keep after taxes.

You input the purchase price, the sale price, the holding period (in months — this determines whether you pay short-term or long-term capital gains rates), your annual income, filing status, and country. The tool then calculates your tax liability, taking into account the bracket-based nature of capital gains taxes.

The holding period distinction matters enormously. Short-term gains — held less than a year — are taxed as ordinary income. Long-term gains get preferential rates, often significantly lower. For someone selling a significant stock position, the difference between short-term and long-term treatment can amount to thousands of dollars. Run it before you sell, not after.

**Use it when:** you're planning to sell stocks, real estate, or other assets and want to know your actual net proceeds — not just the gross gain.

## 7. Debt Snowball Calculator

The debt snowball method is psychologically powerful: you pay off your smallest balance first, regardless of interest rate, and roll the payment you were making on that debt into the next smallest balance. Each win builds momentum. It's not always the mathematically optimal path, but it's often the path people actually follow — because quick wins build habits, and habits build momentum.

This calculator models that strategy precisely. You enter up to five debts, each with a balance, minimum payment, and interest rate, along with your total monthly payment budget. The tool spits out a payoff schedule: the order you'll pay things off, the month each debt will be eliminated, the total interest you'll pay, and the final payoff date.

What makes it useful isn't the math — the math is straightforward. It's the clarity it provides. You can see that your $500 credit card debt will be gone in four months, freeing up $150 to throw at the next debt. That visual progress is what makes the snowball method work for people who've tried raw willpower and failed.

**Use it when:** you have multiple debts and want to see exactly how the snowball method plays out — and whether it's faster than just paying minimums.

## 8. Inflation Calculator

Inflation is the tax you don't see. Every year, the purchasing power of your money erodes slightly. $100 today buys less than $100 bought five years ago. That's inflation, quietly subtracting from everything you save.

This calculator shows you exactly how much. Input a current amount ($1,000), an annual inflation rate (the US average runs around 3%), and a number of years (say, 20). The tool tells you what that $1,000 will be worth in future dollars — and what future dollar amount you'd need to have today's $1,000 in equivalent purchasing power.

At 3% annual inflation, $100 today is worth about $74 in 10 years. In 30 years, it's worth roughly $40. This is why keeping all your money in a savings account paying 1% interest while inflation runs at 3% means you're net losing purchasing power every single year. This isn't financial gloom — it's arithmetic that changes the conversation from "is my money safe?" to "is my money safe enough adjusted for what things actually cost?"

**Use it when:** you want to understand the real return on your savings, evaluate whether a "safe" investment is actually safe in purchasing-power terms, or plan for long-term financial goals.
