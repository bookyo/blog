## 1. Hodgkin-Huxley Neuron — Watch a Neuron Think

The Hodgkin-Huxley model is one of the most beautiful pieces of mathematical biology ever written. It describes how action potentials — the electrical signals your brain uses to communicate — are generated by ion channels in cell membranes.

This interactive visualization simulates it in real time. You get:

- A live membrane potential graph showing the spike and recovery cycle
- Controls for sodium (Na⁺) and potassium (K⁺) conductance
- Adjustable stimulus current to trigger single or repetitive firing

What makes this remarkable isn't just the neuroscience — it's that the entire behavior emerges from 4 coupled differential equations. The spike you see is pure mathematics made physical.

**Use it:** When you want to understand seizure dynamics, neural coding, or why your hand tingles when you hit your funny bone.

## 2. DNA Double Helix — See the Code of Life in 3D

DNA is often described as a "double helix," but textbooks rarely show you *why* it curls, how base pairs snap together, or what happens when you heat it enough to denature.

This interactive 3D visualization lets you rotate, zoom, and inspect the full molecular structure. You can see:

- The sugar-phosphate backbone and how it creates structural tension

This means you can finally understand PCR amplification (the technique behind COVID tests and DNA forensics) at a visceral level.

## 3. Enzyme Kinetics — The Molecular Lock-and-Key in Action

Enzymes are the fastest chemists on Earth. Some catalyze reactions millions of times per second. But how do they work, and why does increasing substrate concentration stop helping after a point?

The Michaelis-Menten plot answers this visually. You get:

- The signature rectangular hyperbolic curve showing reaction velocity vs. substrate concentration
- Controls for Vmax (maximum velocity) and Km (substrate affinity)
- Three inhibition modes: competitive, non-competitive, and uncompetitive

In other words, this shows you why some drugs work by blocking enzymes (competitive inhibition), and why other drugs need to be designed differently.

## 5. Turing Patterns — How Zebras Get Their Stripes

Alan Turing had a wild idea in 1952: biological patterns like zebra stripes, leopard spots, and finger digits might all form through the same mathematical process — reaction-diffusion.

This visualization implements the Gray-Scott model. You get:

- Real-time simulation of two chemicals (an activator and an inhibitor) spreading at different rates
- Controls for feed rate and kill rate — tune these and watch patterns emerge, migrate, or die
- Presets that reproduce spots, stripes, spirals, and maze-like structures

What makes this extraordinary: it shows how a single universal equation can generate the full diversity of animal coat patterns across species.

## 6. Network Contagion (Granovetter's Threshold Model) — Why Some Ideas Go Viral and Others Die

Why did some protests spread across cities in hours while similar movements elsewhere fizzled? Granovetter's threshold model explains it: people join collective action not when they've thought it through, but when they think enough others are already in.

This visualization lets you:

- Assign participation thresholds to individual agents in a network
- Watch how small initial pushes cascade into mass mobilization — or don't
- Explore how network topology determines whether a contagion succeeds

This is the same math used to model financial panics, viral social media trends, and adoption of new technologies.

## 7. Brownian Motion in Colloids — The Random Walk of Life

In 1905, Einstein explained Brownian motion — the jittering of pollen grains suspended in water — using molecular kinetic theory. This was one of the key pieces of evidence that atoms actually exist.

This simulation tracks individual colloidal particles in real time under a virtual microscope. You can:

- Watch particles diffuse and collide with solvent molecules you can't see
- See the displacement distribution converge to a Gaussian over time
- Understand the connection between microscopic randomness and macroscopic diffusion (Einstein's relation: D = kBT / 6πηr)

This is foundational for understanding everything from how ink diffuses in water to how drug molecules navigate your bloodstream.

👉 [Run Brownian Motion Simulation](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/brownian-motion-colloid)

## 8. Heart Curve — The Mathematics Behind Every Heartbeat

The heart shape curve isn't just romantic kitsch — it's a parametric equation that produces one of the most recognizable visual forms in mathematics. This visualization lets you explore it interactively.

This connects to real cardiac physiology: the cardiac action potential has a characteristic spike-and-plateau shape that maps surprisingly well to these curves.
