## 1. Schelling Segregation Model

**Try it:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/schelling-segregation](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/schelling-segregation)

In 1971, Thomas Schelling drew a grid of two-colored dots and asked a simple question: if each dot prefers even a slight majority of its neighbors to be the same color, what happens to the whole system?

The answer shocked economists. Even agents with mild preferences (as low as 30%) self-organize into near-total segregation. Schelling won the 2005 Nobel Prize in Economics for this insight.

**What this means:** Segregation in housing, schools, and workplaces can emerge not from discriminatory policies but from individually rational — even tolerant — preferences. The micro motive doesn't match the macro behavior.

The visualization lets you adjust the similarity threshold, population density, and grid size, then watch the system evolve step by step. Push the threshold above 40% and watch segregation spike toward 90%. Drop it below 25% and the grid stays integrated.

This is one of the most important models in computational social science — and now it's a live sandbox you can play with in seconds.

## 2. Motherhood Penalty & Fatherhood Premium

**Try it:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/motherhood-penalty](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/motherhood-penalty)

Here's a number worth knowing: women lose approximately 7% of their income for every child they have. Men gain about 6% per child. This asymmetry is called the motherhood penalty, and it's one of the most robust findings in labor economics.

The visualization is built around a real case study — "Hong," a physician in Shanghai whose career trajectory diverged sharply from her husband's after their children were born. The interactive career simulation walks through each decision point: the hospital with 3-shift rotations, the foreign company with business trips, the eventual move to a local clinic at a 50% pay cut. All motivated by childcare needs.

**What this means:** The gender pay gap isn't just about equal pay for equal work. A substantial portion of it accumulates over a lifetime as parenthood affects women's career trajectories in ways that compound year after year.

The tool also includes an international comparison across China, the USA, and Europe, plus a personal calculator that estimates your own lifetime earnings impact based on gender, region, number of children, and years since first child.

Researchers featured include Michelle Budig, Shelley Correll, Claudia Goldin (Nobel laureate), and 杨菊华 (Yang Juhua) on the Chinese context.

## 3. Structural Holes — Social Network Brokerage

**Try it:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/structural-holes](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/structural-holes)

Ronald Burt's Structural Holes theory answers a question that sounds simple but has massive implications: why do some people consistently get better opportunities, faster promotions, and more creative ideas than others — even with the same qualifications?

The answer, Burt found, has nothing to do with how many people you know. It has everything to do with whether those people know each other.

When your contacts span disconnected clusters — groups that don't interact with each other — you sit on what Burt calls "structural holes." You become the broker. And brokerage creates two kinds of advantage:

- **Information benefits:** You see things no one in your network can see alone. You get earlier access to opportunities and novel ideas.
- **Control benefits:** You control the flow of information and resources between groups. Neither side has full visibility, giving you leverage.

**What this means:** The same number of contacts can produce vastly different outcomes. Four contacts with only 1 inter-contact tie yield an effective size of 3.5. Four contacts who all know each other yield an effective size of 1.0. Structure beats size.

The visualization lets you build and analyze your own networks, measure effective size, constraint, and redundancy using Burt's formulas, and compare high-holes vs. low-holes configurations side by side.

## 4. The Fifth Consumption Era

**Try it:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/fifth-consumption-era](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/fifth-consumption-era)

Economic historians identify distinct "consumption eras" — long waves where the dominant logic of what people buy and why fundamentally shifts. The Fifth Consumption Era (2021–2043) is the one we're living through right now, and it's defined by a pivot from material accumulation to spiritual fulfillment and well-being.

This visualization maps the transition across 7 dimensions using the 7S framework: Structure, Strategy, Story, Shared Identity, Systems, Settings, and Synergy. It includes a timeline of consumption shifts, demographic breakdowns, and an interactive explorer where you can see how different generations prioritize health, experiences, sustainability, and meaning.

**What this means:** If you're building products or services, the winning category in this era isn't "more stuff." It's anything that helps people feel well, live meaningfully, and connect authentically. The visualization makes this abstract claim concrete by showing the data behind it.

## 5. Two-Sided Markets

**Try it:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/two-sided-markets](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/two-sided-markets)

Uber, Airbnb, App Store, credit cards — what do these businesses have in common? They're all two-sided markets. They have two distinct user groups (buyers and sellers, hosts and guests, developers and users) and the value for each side depends on how many people are on the other side.

This creates a chicken-and-egg problem that has killed many startups. If you need both sides to show up simultaneously, which side do you build first? How do you subsidize one side without destroying your economics?

The visualization simulates cross-network effects, price balancing, and platform tipping dynamics. You can model scenarios like the "rabbit and hare" strategy (subsidize one side heavily to seed the platform) vs. balanced growth.

**What this means:** Two-sided markets are inherently prone to winner-take-all dynamics. Understanding the cross-network effects at play is essential for anyone building platforms — and essential context for understanding why antitrust regulators are increasingly scrutinizing platforms like Apple, Google, and Amazon.

## 6. Narrative Economics

**Try it:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/narrative-economics](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/narrative-economics)

Robert Shiller won the Nobel Prize in 2017 partly for showing that economic fluctuations are driven less by rational calculation than by the stories people tell themselves. When everyone believes a recession is coming, their belief itself becomes the recession's cause — through reduced spending, hiring freezes, and investment pullbacks.

This visualization explores how economic narratives spread, mutate, and reach epidemic thresholds. It models the SIR framework (Susceptible → Infected → Recovered) applied to narrative contagion in financial markets.

You can see how a single viral post about a bank failure can cascade through social networks, how confirmation bias causes narrative mutations to spread faster than corrections, and how some narratives are "stickier" than others because they align with existing worldviews.

**What this means:** Rational actor models are necessary but not sufficient for understanding markets. The stories people believe — about inflation, about tech bubbles, about job security — directly shape economic outcomes. Narrative is not a distraction from economics. It is economics.
