## 8 Free Force & Motion Visualizations That Make Physics Actually Click

Physics teachers have been drawing arrows on whiteboards for centuries. Now you can manipulate those arrows yourself.

These 8 free interactive visualizations let you adjust forces, watch collisions play out in real-time, and build intuition for concepts that are notoriously hard to visualize. All run directly in your browser — no downloads, no sign-ups, no plugins.

---

### 1. Elastic & Inelastic Collision Simulator

**https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/collision**

When two billiard balls smash together, where does the energy go? This simulator lets you set masses, initial velocities, and restitution coefficients for both 1D and 2D collisions — then watch momentum and kinetic energy play out frame by frame.

You can dial from perfectly elastic (e=1, no energy lost) to completely inelastic (e=0, objects stick together). The live charts track momentum and velocity over time, so you can see conservation laws in action rather than just reading about them.

Use this when: you need to grok why a ping-pong ball bounces better than a clay blob, or when you're debugging a game physics engine and collisions feel "off."

### 2. Force Composition — Parallelogram Method

**https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/force-composition**

Two kids pull on a wagon with ropes at different angles. How do you find the net force? The answer is the parallelogram rule — and this visualization shows you exactly why, with real-time vector arithmetic you can manipulate.

Drag force vectors, watch the parallelogram update, and see the resultant change as you adjust magnitude and direction. The component values update live.

Use this when: you're designing a crane rigging system, calculating loads in structural engineering, or just trying to finally understand why your physics textbook drew those diagonal arrows.

---

### 3. Force Resolution — Breaking Forces Into Components

**https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/force-resolution**

A hill pushes on a sled with 200N of force. How much of that pushes the sled down the slope vs. pressing it into the hill? This is force resolution — the inverse of composition — and it's one of the most confusing skills in intro physics.

This simulator decomposes any force into rectangular components using trigonometry. Adjust the force direction and magnitude, watch the parallel and perpendicular components shift in real-time, and apply it to a pendulum scenario.

Use this when: you keep mixing up "component along the slope" vs. "component perpendicular to the slope," or when you're building a ramp and need to know the actual downward force.

### 4. Angular Momentum Conservation

**https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/angular-momentum-conservation**

Figure skaters don't just spin faster because they try harder — they use physics. Arms out means slow rotation. Arms in means blazing fast. The angular momentum stays constant; the rotational kinetic energy comes from muscle work.

This visualization demonstrates conservation of angular momentum using a rotating platform. Adjust the moment of inertia by moving masses closer or farther from the axis, and watch angular velocity change in real-time. The system tracks both L (angular momentum) and KE (rotational kinetic energy) numerically.

Use this when: you want to understand why divers tuck during somersaults, or why longboarders lean into turns.

### 5. Coriolis Force — Why Hurricanes Spin

**https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/coriolis-force**

YouTube comments about which way toilets flush are famously wrong. The Coriolis force is too weak at small scales to determine toilet flush direction — but at planetary scales, it controls everything from hurricane rotation to why subcontinental missiles need trajectory corrections.

This visualization shows Earth's rotation from a top-down polar view. Launch projectiles and watch them curve relative to the surface depending on latitude. It also covers the Foucault pendulum and atmospheric circulation cells.

Use this when: you need to finally settle the "do toilets flush differently in each hemisphere" debate, or when you're building a flight planning tool and need to understand wind patterns.

### 6. Newton's Law of Cooling

**https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/newton-cooling-law**

A cup of coffee at 95°C sits in a 20°C room. How long until it's drinkable? Newton's Law of Cooling says the rate of temperature change is proportional to the temperature difference with the environment — which produces an exponential decay curve.

This simulator lets you set initial temperature, ambient temperature, and the cooling constant. Watch the temperature curve develop in real-time. You can also find the time to reach a target temperature directly from the curve.

Use this when: you're calculating how long to leave a roast out after cooking, determining safe food storage times, or modeling any first-order thermal system.

### 7. Center of Mass Motion

**https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/center-of-mass-motion**

When two astronauts push off each other in empty space, their center of mass continues on the same trajectory as before the push. This is why rocket stages don't "lose" the vehicle's orbital path when expended.

This visualization tracks the center of mass of multi-body systems. Set masses and initial velocities, watch the system evolve, and see the center of mass maintain its inertial path regardless of internal movements.

Use this when: you're designing a multi-stage launcher, analyzing collision trajectories, or teaching conservation of momentum in systems where mass changes.

### 8. Collision Lab — 1D and 2D Momentum

**https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/collision**

This is the more advanced sibling of the basic collision tool. It handles both 1D and 2D collisions, shows you the full momentum vector diagrams, and lets you set the coefficient of restitution precisely. The energy lost in each collision is computed and displayed.

Use this when: you're building a game engine and need to verify your elastic vs. inelastic collision handling, or when you're running a physics lab and need to check whether your data matches theory.

## Why These Matter

Physics is one of those subjects where reading about something and watching it happen are fundamentally different experiences. A whiteboard diagram can show you that momentum is conserved in a collision. But watching a chart track both momentum lines staying flat while kinetic energy drops — that creates a different kind of understanding.

What most textbooks still don't solve: the gap between understanding a formula and building intuition for what it means when variables change. These 8 visualizations are a start at bridging that gap.

All tools are free, run in-browser, and require no installation. Bookmark this page — you'll come back.
