## 4. Rose Chart Generator — History's Most Famous Data Visualization, Revived

Florence Nightingale didn't just treat patients. She invented data visualization as a political tool.

In the 1850s, she showed British military leaders that soldiers were dying more from preventable disease than battlefield wounds. Her weapon? The rose chart (then called a coxcomb chart). By encoding both the angle *and* area of each segment, she made the case for sanitation reform irrefutable.

The ElysiaTools Rose Chart Generator brings this powerful technique into the modern era. You get the historical aesthetic (including a "vintage" color scheme) alongside modern features: tooltips, percentage labels, animation, and multiple color schemes.

This means you can make a chart that is statistically rigorous *and* visually arresting. Data that moves people tends to drive action.

Use it when: You have categorical data with periodic cycles and want a visualization that stands out from generic bar charts.

## 5. Chord Diagram Generator — Map the Relationships You Can't See

Some of the most interesting data isn't about values — it's about *connections*.

Migration patterns between countries. Trade flows between industries. Communication volume between teams. These are bidirectional relationships that a bar chart simply can't capture.

Chord diagrams solve this. They arrange entities in a circle and draw arcs between them, with the arc thickness indicating relationship strength. The result is a map of flows that reveals clusters, outliers, and unexpected connections.

The ElysiaTools Chord Diagram Generator accepts JSON data with source-target-value pairs, supports custom color schemes, and can display statistics like total outflow and inflow per entity.

This means network relationships become visible — not just "what happened" but "who is connected to whom."

Use it when: You need to visualize bidirectional flows between more than two entities.

The sunburst chart solves this with a simple idea: encode hierarchy radially. The center is the root. Each ring outward represents a deeper level. Click a segment to zoom in. The whole hierarchy stays navigable.

## The Problem These Tools Don't Yet Solve

These 7 tools handle the "what" and "when" of data visualization well. But they stop at the chart level.

The harder question is: **how do you build a coherent data story across multiple charts?**

Most tools let you export one chart at a time. Building a dashboard — a connected sequence of visualizations where one chart's filter drives another's data — still requires code or expensive BI platforms.

That's the gap worth filling. A tool that lets you chain these visualizations together, pass a selection from one chart as a filter to the next, and export the whole sequence as a single interactive story — that's the next level.

Until then, start with one chart. Pick your most interesting dataset. Paste it in. You'll be surprised what you see that you couldn't see before.
