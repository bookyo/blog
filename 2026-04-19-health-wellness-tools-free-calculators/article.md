## Know Your Numbers: The Body Composition Tools

### 1. BMI Calculator

Body Mass Index is the most misunderstood metric in health. Doctors use it. Diet plans reference it. Most people know it exists but have no idea what it actually measures—or that it has real limitations.

BMI divides your weight by the square of your height. That's it. It doesn't distinguish between muscle and fat, which is why a bodybuilder can register as "obese" on the chart. What it *does* give you is a useful population-level snapshot, especially when combined with other data points.

The [BMI Calculator on ElysiaTools](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/bmi-calculator) goes beyond the number. It returns your health category (Underweight / Normal / Overweight / Obese), your ideal weight range based on your height, and personalized recommendations. You can toggle between metric and imperial units, and optionally add your age and gender for a more nuanced risk assessment.

Check this number first—but keep reading.

### 2. BMR Calculator

BMR stands for Basal Metabolic Rate: the calories your body burns at complete rest. Not exercise. Not walking to the kitchen. Just keeping your heart beating, your lungs breathing, and your brain running.

For a typical 30-year-old male at 70kg and 175cm, BMR lands around 1,600–1,700 calories per day. Eat below that for weeks and your body doesn't just lose weight—it slows everything down, cannibalizes muscle, and makes regain almost inevitable once you eat normally again.

The [BMR Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/bmr-calculator) lets you choose between three formulas: Mifflin-St Jeor (most accurate for general use), Harris-Benedict (the older standard), and Katch-McArdle (requires body fat percentage). You get your exact number, a plain-English explanation, and a note on what it means for your daily calorie needs—which is significantly higher than BMR itself.

This number should anchor every nutrition plan you ever attempt.

### 4. Body Fat Calculator (US Navy)

If BMI is a blunt instrument, the US Navy body fat formula is a scalpel. It's the same method the US military uses to estimate body composition, requiring only three measurements: height, neck circumference, and waist. For women, hip circumference is added.

The formula isn't perfect—calipers and DEXA scans are more accurate—but it requires zero equipment beyond a measuring tape and delivers results close enough to be useful. You get your estimated body fat percentage, a category (Essential Fat / Athletes / Fitness / Average / Obese), and a lean mass vs. fat mass breakdown.

The [US Navy Body Fat Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/body-fat-calculator-us-navy) is particularly valuable alongside the BMI Calculator. A "normal" BMI with high body fat is far more common than obesity—and far more dangerous. These two numbers together give you a picture that neither provides alone.

### 5. One Rep Max Calculator

If you lift weights and don't know your 1RM, you're probably training with the wrong loads.

Your one-rep max (1RM) is the maximum weight you can lift for a single repetition. Every meaningful training percentage is derived from it: 85% for hypertrophy, 70–75% for strength endurance, 60–65% for muscular endurance. Wing it and you're guessing.

The [One Rep Max Calculator](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/one-rep-max) accepts any weight and reps you can perform comfortably—say, 100kg for 5 reps—and extrapolates your 1RM using three different formulas (Epley, Brzycki, Lander). It also returns a full percentage table so you can immediately program your next session.

You don't need to attempt a dangerous max lift to know your numbers. You just need two data points from a set you already do.

## Calm Down: The Mental Wellness Tools

Body composition data is only half the picture. Chronic stress destroys sleep quality, weakens immune function, degrades decision-making, and—research increasingly shows—drives dangerous visceral fat storage. These three tools address the mental side of health.

### 6. 4-7-8 Breathing Exercise

Dr. Andrew Weil popularized this technique based on ancient yogic breathing. Inhale for 4 seconds, hold for 7, exhale for 8. Repeat for 4–8 cycles. The result is a measurable reduction in heart rate and cortisol within minutes.

The [ElysiaTools 4-7-8 Breathing Exercise](https://elysiatools.com/en/tools/breathing-4-7-8) is interactive. You can customize cycle count, phase durations, and toggle sound on or off.

Most people try this once, feel something shift, and never use it again. They treat it as a novelty rather than a skill. Four cycles takes under 80 seconds. Use it before a high-stakes meeting or 20 minutes before bed—and you'll have hard data on how your nervous system responds to a practiced signal versus an improvised one.
