## 2. Double Pendulum — Where Determinism Breaks Down

Two hinges. Two weights. One formula that Newton himself could have written.

And yet the double pendulum is chaotic. Give it the same starting position twice and you'll get two completely different swings after just a few seconds. This isn't randomness — it's *deterministic chaos*. The equations are exact. The outcome is not.

This is the visualization that makes chaos theory visceral. Flip between Lagrangian and Hamiltonian views. Watch the phase space blow up.

**Use it when:** You want to understand why "simple systems can behave impossibly complex."

## 4. Logistic Map — The Road from Order to Chaos

Watch a single population model make the transition from stable cycles to pure chaos — with no randomness involved.

The logistic map describes population growth with a feedback loop: `x → r·x·(1-x)`. When you dial up the growth rate `r`, the system starts cycling predictably. Then it doubles. Then doubles again. Then — abruptly — it becomes chaotic. Bump `r` just slightly and you get completely different behavior.

This period-doubling route to chaos is found everywhere: fluid dynamics, heart rhythms, climate systems. The logistic map is the clearest window into it.

**Use it when:** You need to explain chaos theory to someone without losing them.

**[Try it →](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/logistic-map)**

## 5. Cellular Automata (Rule 30) — Complexity From Simplicity

Start with a row of cells, all black except one white cell in the center. Apply one binary rule to generate the next row. Repeat.

That's it. That's Rule 30.

And yet the pattern it produces looks indistinguishable from randomness. Stephen Wolfram, the founder of Wolfram Research, used Rule 30 to argue that simple programs can generate all the complexity we associate with nature. It's also the basis for cellular automata-based random number generators.

**Use it when:** You're exploring emergence — how complexity arises from simple rules.

## 6. Brownian Motion — Einstein's Proof of Atoms

In 1905, Einstein wrote four papers. One proposed that atoms exist. The evidence? Brownian motion.

Tiny pollen grains jiggling in water had puzzled scientists for decades. Einstein showed that the erratic movement was caused by water molecules bombarding the particles — and his equations correctly predicted the diffusion coefficient from observable data. Jean Perrin confirmed it experimentally in 1908, ending a century-old debate about atomic theory.

This visualization simulates the motion, shows the random walk statistics, and demonstrates how the math matches real physical behavior.

**Use it when:** You're teaching probability, statistics, or the history of physics.

## 7. Bayes' Theorem — The Math of Updating Beliefs

A patient tests positive for a rare disease. How worried should they be?

The answer depends on the base rate. A 99% accurate test for a disease affecting 1 in 1,000 people means most positive results are still false positives. Bayes' Theorem captures exactly this intuition — that the same evidence should lead you to different conclusions depending on what you believed before.

This visualization walks through worked scenarios, updating probabilities step by step. It's the clearest interactive explanation of Bayesian reasoning you'll find.

**Use it when:** You want to understand why prior probability matters so much in data science and decision-making.

## 8. Entropy — Why Time Flows in One Direction

Break an egg. Mix cream into coffee. These things happen. They don't un-happen.

Entropy — the second law of thermodynamics — explains why. A closed system tends toward disorder over time. But the definition is subtle: entropy isn't "messiness." It's a precise statistical property of microstates. This visualization lets you manipulate the parameters and watch the statistical mechanics unfold.

The mind-bending part: locally, life and order exist by *increasing* entropy elsewhere. Every time you clean your desk, you're making the air around you slightly more disordered.

**Use it when:** You want to deeply understand the second law — not just memorize it.
