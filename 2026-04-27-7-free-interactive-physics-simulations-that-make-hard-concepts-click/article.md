## 2. Wave Superposition — Where Two Waves Become One

When two waves meet, they don't collide—they *add*. That's the principle of superposition.

This simulation lets you overlap two waves with independent frequency, amplitude, and phase controls. Crank both frequencies close together and watch the beat pattern emerge—the amplitude pulsing at f_beat = |f₁ - f₂|.

**What you'll explore:** Constructive interference (waves add up) vs. destructive interference (waves cancel out). The beat frequency formula that's the foundation of how tuners work.

**Use it for:** Finally understanding why noise-canceling headphones actually work.

## 3. Doppler Effect — Why Sirens Sound Weird When They Pass You

When an ambulance drives toward you, its siren sounds higher. As it passes and drives away, it drops to a lower pitch. That's the Doppler effect.

This simulation visualizes the compression of wavefronts ahead of a moving source. Adjust source velocity and watch the wavelength literally squash in front and stretch behind. Real-time frequency readouts show the exact shift.

**What you'll explore:** The f' = f₀·v/(v±vₛ formula in action. Why approaching sources blue-shift and receding sources red-shift.

**Use it for:** Explaining radar guns, ultrasound blood flow measurement, and why distant galaxies are red-shifted (which told us the universe is expanding).

## 4. Simple Pendulum — The Gateway Drug to Harmonic Motion

A pendulum swinging back and forth looks simple. But it encodes one of the most important concepts in all of physics: harmonic motion.

This simulation shows the pendulum in real time with live angle, velocity, and energy charts. Crank the initial angle past 15° and watch the motion stop being sinusoidal—the small-angle approximation breaks down right before your eyes.

**What you'll explore:** The transition from linear to nonlinear. Energy conservation (KE + PE = constant) in a frictionless system. Damping when you add it.

**Use it for:** Building intuition for oscillations before you encounter them in circuits, quantum mechanics, and climate models.

## 5. Damped Harmonic Oscillator — Why Your Car Doesn't Bounce Forever

Tie a mass to a spring and it bounces. But real springs don't bounce forever—each bounce is smaller than the last. That's damping.

This simulation models a spring-mass-damper system with three damping regimes: underdamped (bounces, slowly settling), critically damped (fastest return without bouncing), and overdamped (too much friction, returns sluggishly). The phase space plot spirals inward as energy bleeds away as heat.

**What you'll explore:** Why car shock absorbers are critically damped. The damping ratio ζ and how it determines system behavior. Exponential decay in displacement: x(t) ∝ e^(-ζω₀t).

**Use it for:** Understanding why earthquakes shake buildings with decreasing amplitude. Or why a door with a pneumatic closer doesn't slam.

**Try it:** [Damped Harmonic Oscillator Simulator →](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/spring-oscillator)

## 6. Standing Wave — The Physics Inside Every Musical Instrument

Tie a rope to a door handle, flick it, and watch a wave travel, reflect, and overlap with itself. Sometimes the overlaps produce a wave that *doesn't travel*—it just sits there, pulsing in place. That's a standing wave.

This simulation shows incident and reflected waves combining into a standing wave. Select harmonics and watch nodes (points of zero amplitude) and antinodes (points of maximum vibration) lock into position. The nth harmonic always has n+1 nodes.

**What you'll explore:** Why a guitar string's 2nd harmonic has twice the frequency of its fundamental. Why organ pipes behave differently from guitar strings. The f_n = n × f₁ formula that governs every musical instrument.

**Use it for:** Finally understanding why instruments produce distinct timbres, even when playing the same note.

**Try it:** [Standing Wave Simulator →](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/standing-wave)

## 7. Double Slit Quantum Trajectory — The Experiment That Broke Reality

Fire electrons one at a time through two slits. Each electron arrives at a discrete point on the screen—classical particle behavior. But after many electrons accumulate, an interference pattern emerges—a wave phenomenon.

This simulation lets you fire individual electrons and watch them accumulate. Toggle on the path detector and watch the interference pattern vanish—because measuring which slit the electron went through *destroys* the quantum superposition.

**What you'll explore:** Wave-particle duality. How measurement in quantum mechanics isn't passive observation—it's an active process that changes outcomes. The probability amplitude framework P = |ψ₁ + ψ₂|².

**Use it for:** Understanding why quantum computing relies on maintaining coherence, and why quantum cryptography is fundamentally secure.
