## 1. Hénon Map — The Strange Attractor That Looks Like a Fractal Wave

The [Hénon map](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/henon-map) is one of the simplest systems that produces genuine chaos. It was introduced by French astronomer Michel Hénon in 1966 as a simplified model of the Poincaré section for the Lorenz system.

Two coupled equations iterate thousands of times, each point spawning the next. The result is a shape that looks hand-drawn but is completely deterministic — and endlessly complex.

**Why it matters:** The Hénon map demonstrates how simple quadratic equations can produce fractal structures. The attractor has a Hausdorff dimension of approximately 1.261, meaning it's "more than a line but less than a plane." It lives in fractional dimensions.

**Use it for:** Understanding how discrete dynamical systems generate fractal geometry, exploring the boundary between stability and chaos.

## 2. Tent Map — The Simplest Road to Chaos

The [tent map](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/tent-map) earns its name from its triangular shape. It folds and stretches space in the simplest possible way — and in doing so, reveals exactly how chaos emerges from simple rules.

Feed in any starting value. Watch it iterate. For most parameters, the system settles into a fixed point or orbit. But as you tune the parameter, the system suddenly **period-doubles** — then period-doubles again — then again — until the periods become infinite and the system goes chaotic.

**Why it matters:** The tent map is the clearest visualization of the **period-doubling route to chaos** — the same route observed in weather systems, electronic circuits, and population models.

**Use it for:** Seeing exactly how deterministic systems transition from order to chaos without any randomness involved.

## 3. Double Pendulum — Where Determinism Meets Unpredictability

The [double pendulum](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/double-pendulum) is the most famous example of chaotic dynamics in the physical world. Two hinged rods, both free to swing in two dimensions. That's it. That's the entire system.

Give it any two starting angles. Run the simulation twice with microscopically different starting conditions. The first time, the endpoints trace one pattern. The second time — a completely different pattern. Both are correct. Both are deterministic. Neither is predictable beyond a few seconds.

**Why it matters:** The double pendulum demonstrates **sensitive dependence on initial conditions** — the core signature of chaos. It's also Hamiltonian, meaning it conserves energy while producing chaos — a profound contradiction.

**Use it for:** Understanding why we can't predict the weather beyond ~10 days, even with perfect equations and infinite computing power.

## 4. Forced Pendulum — When Period-Doubling Leads to Chaos

The [forced pendulum](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/forced-pendulum) takes the classic pendulum and adds an external periodic driving force. As you increase the drive strength, the pendulum's motion undergoes a series of bifurcations — from simple oscillation to period-2, period-4, period-8, and finally, chaos.

What makes this powerful is that you can **watch the bifurcation diagram** — a map of all the periodicities the system can exhibit as the driving force changes.

**Why it matters:** The forced pendulum is experimentally realizable. Build it with a real pendulum, drive it with a motor, and you'll see the same period-doubling cascade. The mathematics isn't just theoretical — it's measurable in physical reality.

**Use it for:** Exploring the boundary between predictable and unpredictable behavior, understanding how engineering systems can suddenly go chaotic.

## 5. Lorenz Attractor — The Butterfly That Changed Everything

The [Lorenz attractor](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/lorenz-attractor) is the icon of chaos theory. Edward Lorenz discovered it accidentally in 1963 while running a weather simulation. He rounded a variable from 0.506127 to 0.506 and re-ran the code. The result was a completely different weather pattern.

The attractor — that butterfly shape traced in 3D space — is not a curve. It's a surface. And the trajectory that draws it never crosses itself, never repeats, yet stays confined to that shape forever.

**Why it matters:** Lorenz's discovery gave chaos theory its most powerful metaphor. The attractor shows that **long-term prediction is fundamentally impossible** even in purely deterministic systems — not because we lack precision, but because the system is structurally sensitive.

**Use it for:** Grasping why chaos theory was a paradigm shift in science — not just a mathematical curiosity, but a new understanding of how nature works.

## 6. Feigenbaum Constant — The Universal Number Hidden in Every Chaotic System

The [Feigenbaum constant visualization](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/feigenbaum-constant) reveals one of the most surprising facts in mathematics: every system that undergoes period-doubling on the route to chaos converges to the same rate.

That rate is **4.669201609...** — the Feigenbaum constant. It appears in the logistic map, the Hénon map, the tent map, electronic circuits, fluid dynamics, and lasers. The same number, everywhere.

**Why it matters:** The Feigenbaum constant tells us that chaos isn't just common — it's **universal**. Different physical systems, described by different equations, share the same mathematical fingerprint on the road to chaos. This is a profound statement about the unity of mathematics across disciplines.

**Use it for:** Seeing how specific numerical constants can characterize entire categories of physical behavior — and why mathematicians call this "universality."

## 7. Lyapunov Exponent — Measuring How Fast Uncertainty Grows

The [Lyapunov exponent](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/lyapunov-exponent) quantifies chaos with a single number. A positive Lyapunov exponent means nearby trajectories in a dynamical system diverge exponentially — chaos. A negative exponent means the system is stable and predictable.

This is the **mathematical measure** of the butterfly effect: how quickly a tiny uncertainty about initial conditions gets amplified into total unpredictability.

**Why it matters:** The Lyapunov exponent gives chaos a number. It's not just "chaotic" or "not chaotic" — it's *how chaotic*, measured precisely in bits of information per unit time. Weather systems typically have a Lyapunov exponent of about 0.965 nepers per day.

**Use it for:** Comparing different chaotic systems quantitatively, understanding the information-theoretic foundations of chaos.

## 8. Poincaré Section — Slicing Through Chaos to Find Hidden Structure

The [Poincaré section](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/poincare-section) is a technique, not a single system — and it's arguably the most powerful tool in chaos theory. Take a continuous 3D trajectory and record only where it crosses a particular 2D plane. Discard all the in-between data.

What you get is a **stroboscopic snapshot** of the dynamics. Regular motion produces simple patterns on the section. Chaos produces complex, fractal-like structures. Resonant motion produces chains of islands.

**Why it matters:** Poincaré sections were Henri Poincaré's key insight in 1890 — decades before computers made chaos theory tractable. They remain the standard way to detect chaos in experimental data from physical systems.

**Use it for:** Analyzing real-world chaotic data (heart rhythms, EEGs, stock prices), distinguishing chaos from noise, finding periodic windows inside chaotic regimes.
