## 1. Fifth Consumption Era — See How Societies Change Their Shopping Habits

**URL:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/fifth-consumption-era](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/fifth-consumption-era)

Every generation shops differently. Japan's Atsushi Miura identified five distinct "consumption eras." We're now entering the fifth — and it's a radical departure from everything before it.

The Fifth Consumption Era (2021–2043) isn't about buying more stuff. It's about buying *meaning*. As societies age, birth rates drop, and single-person households become the norm, consumption is shifting from material accumulation to spiritual fulfillment, community belonging, and sustainability.

**What makes this visualization special:** It's built around Miura's 7S Framework — seven dimensions that define how people in this era spend their money:

- **Slow** — From fast fashion to handmade, artisanal, and vintage
- **Small** — 15-minute walkable communities instead of mega-malls
- **Sociable** — Real human connections over brand loyalty
- **Soft** — Walking and cycling lifestyles
- **Sustainable** — Circular economy and ethical sourcing
- **Sensuous** — Wellness, mindfulness, and aesthetic experiences
- **Solution** — Social enterprises solving real problems

You can drag sliders to weight each dimension, compare preset scenarios (Japan Current vs. China Trend), and watch radar charts update in real time. There's even a Maslow's Hierarchy comparison tab that shows how well-being priorities have shifted.

**Use case:** If you're in marketing, urban planning, or product design, understanding which S your target demographic scores high on isn't guesswork — it's a framework.

## 2. Narrative Economics — How Economic Ideas Spread Like Epidemics

**URL:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/narrative-economics](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/narrative-economics)

Nobel laureate Robert Shiller proposed that economic events are driven not just by fundamentals, but by the stories people tell about them. Think about it: the 2008 financial crisis spread as fast as it did partly because everyone kept comparing it to 1929. Bitcoin's 2017 boom wasn't just about technology — it was about the narrative of "digital gold" and "get rich quick."

Narrative Economics applies the classic **SIR model** (Susceptible → Infected → Recovered) to how economic ideas spread through society.

**How it works:**

- **S (Susceptible)** — People who haven't yet heard the narrative
- **I (Infected)** — Active spreaders of the narrative
- **R (Recovered)** — People who've lost interest or become immune

You set the **contagion rate** (how fast the idea spreads) and **recovery rate** (how fast people tire of it), then watch the epidemic curve play out on screen. The **R₀ value** tells you whether you're looking at an epidemic or an idea that dies out quietly.

**What makes this visualization special:** It comes with real historical case studies — the Laffer Curve narrative of the 1980s, the Great Recession comparison to 1929, and Bitcoin's 2017 surge — all modeled with actual parameters. You can explore SIRS mode for narratives that recur (like "housing bubble" warnings that pop up every few years).

There's also a phase plane tab for the mathematically curious, where you can click anywhere on the graph to set initial conditions and watch trajectories unfold.

**Use case:** Trying to understand whether the current "AI bubble" narrative will fade or persist? This tool gives you the intuition, even if the future isn't predictable.

## 3. Two-Sided Markets — Why Your Dating App Subsidizes You

**URL:** [https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/two-sided-markets](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/two-sided-markets)

Here's a puzzle: why does Tinder let you swipe for free? Why does Uber subsidize both riders and drivers during growth phases? Why does Google give you search for nothing while businesses pay billions to appear nearby?

Welcome to **two-sided markets** — platforms where value is created by connecting two (or more) distinct user groups, and each side's value depends on the other side's participation.

**The core insight:** In two-sided markets, pricing isn't based on cost. It's based on **elasticity** — which side cares more about price. The more price-sensitive side gets subsidized (or even paid to join). The less price-sensitive side subsidizes them.

Think about it: a new dating app needs women more than men (men have higher elasticity — they'll leave if it's too expensive). So the app makes it free for women and charges men, or offers men heavy subsidies early on. This is called **skewed pricing**.

**What makes this visualization special:** You can:

- Add and remove users from Group A and Group B and watch connection counts update live
- Run a **Growth Flywheel simulation** that shows how platforms escape the "chicken and egg" problem and reach critical mass
- Explore **skewed pricing scenarios** with presets for real platforms: Taobao, Uber, Visa, PlayStation, Google
- See the mathematical formulas rendered beautifully with KaTeX

The education tab alone is worth the visit — it traces the theory from ancient bazaars to modern super-apps, with Jean Tirole's Nobel Prize-winning framework explained clearly.

**Use case:** If you're building a platform, or trying to understand why your favorite app pricing works the way it does, this visualization builds genuine intuition.
