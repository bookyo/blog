## 1. [Center of Mass Motion](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/center-of-mass-motion)

Two objects orbiting their shared center of mass — that's what this shows. You can adjust masses, initial positions, and velocities, then watch the system evolve.

**Why it matters:** In any closed system, the center of mass moves at constant velocity regardless of what individual parts do. This is why a spinning firework's center of mass follows a perfect parabola even as fragments scatter everywhere.

This means understanding center of mass lets you predict system-wide motion without tracking every particle.

**Use it when:** Studying orbital mechanics, rocket staging, or collisions.

## 2. [Force Composition](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/force-composition)

## 3. [Force Resolution](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/force-resolution)

Opposite of composition: break a single force into perpendicular components. The classic use case is a weight on an inclined plane — gravity pulls *down*, but the object slides *along* the slope. Why?

This visualization decomposes the force vector using trigonometry so you can see exactly how much drives the block down the incline versus pressing it into the surface.

**Use it when:** Working through inclined plane problems or decomposing applied forces in any direction.

## 4. [Collision Simulator](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/collision)

Elastic? Inelastic? Perfectly inelastic? One slider changes the coefficient of restitution, and you watch kinetic energy and momentum play out in real time.

**Why it matters:** Momentum is always conserved in collisions — but kinetic energy only conserves in elastic ones. This is why car crumple zones are designed to absorb energy rather than transmit it. A perfectly inelastic collision (objects stick together) converts the most kinetic energy into deformation and heat.

**Use it when:** Studying momentum conservation, car crash analysis, or billiard ball physics.

## 5. [Archimedes' Principle](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/archimedes-principle)

Buoyancy isn't about weight — it's about *displacement*. Drop an object into a fluid and watch the displaced volume rise. The buoyant force equals the weight of that displaced fluid, regardless of the object's density.

**Why it matters:** A steel ship floats because its shape displaces enough water to balance its weight. A steel nail sinks because it doesn't. This visualization lets you drop objects of different densities and volumes into different fluids to see exactly when they float, sink, or hover in equilibrium.

**Use it when:** Understanding buoyancy, designing flotation devices, or studying fluid statics.

## 6. [Pascal's Law](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/pascal-law)

Pressure applied to an enclosed fluid transmits undiminished to every portion of the fluid and the walls. Change the input force on a small piston, and the output piston — regardless of its size — experiences exactly that same pressure.

This means a small force on a small area can produce a large force on a large area. That's how hydraulic lifts hoist cars.

**Why it matters:** Hydraulic brakes, construction equipment, and airplane control surfaces all depend on Pascal's law. One foot on a brake pedal can stop a 4,000-pound car.

**Use it when:** Understanding hydraulic systems, studying pressure transmission, or designing mechanical advantage systems.

---

## 7. [Angular Momentum Conservation](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/angular-momentum-conservation)

Spin a wheel. Now hold its axis horizontal. The system starts to precess — the wheel doesn't just fall, it traces a slow circle. That's angular momentum doing its thing.

This visualization lets you set initial angular velocities, add or remove spinning masses, and watch the system conserve angular momentum (L = I·ω) in real time.

**Why it matters:** Figure skaters spin faster when they pull their arms in — they reduce their moment of inertia, so their angular velocity must increase to keep angular momentum constant. The same principle keeps gyroscopes stable and satellites oriented.

**Use it when:** Studying rotational dynamics, gyroscopic stability, or Olympic figure skating physics.

---

## 8. [Coriolis Force](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/coriolis-force)

Objects moving over Earth's surface appear to curve — not because a mysterious force acts on them, but because the Earth is rotating beneath them. Switch between an inertial frame and Earth's rotating frame to see the difference.

**Why it matters:** Coriolis deflection explains why hurricanes spin counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and clockwise in the South. It's also why artillery calculations must account for Earth's rotation — a shell fired 20 kilometers drifts about 30 meters sideways.

**Use it when:** Studying meteorology, oceanography, or long-range ballistics.
