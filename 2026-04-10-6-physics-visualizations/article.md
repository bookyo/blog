## 1. Double Pendulum Chaos — Where Determinism Hits its Limits

A double pendulum is two pendulums attached end to end. Simple setup. Chaotic outcome.

What makes it fascinating: tiny differences in starting conditions explode into wildly different trajectories. The same system swings regularly or flips unpredictably — and you can't tell which until you run it. Edward Lorenz discovered the same phenomenon in 1963 while running weather simulations on a computer. Small rounding errors produced dramatically different forecasts. He called it the butterfly effect. The double pendulum is the simplest mechanical system that exhibits it.

Fire individual electrons through two slits. One at a time. You get an interference pattern — as if each electron went through both slits simultaneously.

## 3. Doppler Effect — Why Sirens Sound Different Approaching vs. Receding

You know the pitch shifts. But most people don't realize how extreme it gets.

The visualization shows wave fronts compress ahead of a moving source and stretch behind it. You control the source speed relative to the speed of sound — push past Mach 1 and watch the shock wave form in real time. The sonic boom isn't a footnote. It's the main event.

Christian Doppler described this in 1842 while studying binary stars. He predicted approaching stars would appear bluer and receding ones redder. He was right. Today, astronomers use the same principle to measure how fast 4.2 million galaxies are moving away from us. The cosmic redshift is the Doppler effect operating at the largest scale we can observe.

## 5. Photoelectric Effect — Einstein's Experiment That Won the Nobel

Classical physics predicted brighter light would produce more energetic electrons. The photoelectric effect showed the opposite: electron energy depends on frequency, not intensity.

Below a certain threshold frequency, no electrons escape regardless of brightness. Above it, electrons pop out instantly. Einstein's explanation was radical: light itself is quantized. Energy comes in discrete packets called photons. This was the birth of quantum mechanics — and it earned Einstein the 1921 Nobel Prize in Physics. Not for relativity. For quantizing light.

The ElysiaTools visualization recreates the experiment. Adjust frequency and intensity. Measure the stopping voltage. Watch current respond to threshold frequency. The quantum nature of light isn't stated. It's demonstrated.
