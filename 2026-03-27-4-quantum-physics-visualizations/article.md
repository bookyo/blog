## The Quantum World Is Weirder Than You Think

If you throw a ball at a wall, it bounces back. Every time. That's classical physics — predictable, intuitive, boring.

Now imagine a ball that sometimes passes through the wall. Not because it found a crack. Not because it's small. But because quantum mechanics says walls aren't really walls.

That's quantum tunneling — one of four interactive visualizations that will reshape how you think about the universe at its smallest scales.

These four free tools let you explore quantum mechanics visually, without a physics degree:

## 1. Quantum Harmonic Oscillator

A particle trapped in a bowl-shaped potential that it can never escape classically — yet quantum mechanics says it has a non-zero chance of appearing outside.

The harmonic oscillator is one of quantum mechanics' most important model systems. Why?

**Energy levels are equally spaced.** The formula Eₙ = (n + ½)ħω means adjacent levels differ by exactly ħω, every single time. This unique property makes it exactly solvable and appears everywhere from molecular vibrations to quantum field theory.

The visualization shows:
- Wave functions shaped by Hermite polynomials
- Probability density |ψ|² — where you're most likely to find the particle
- **Zero-point energy** — even at absolute zero, the particle still moves. It can never be completely still.

Try it at [Quantum Harmonic Oscillator](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/quantum-harmonic-oscillator).

## 2. Quantum Tunneling

Classically forbidden. Quantum mechanically routine.

A particle with less energy than a barrier's height still has a non-zero chance of appearing on the other side. This isn't theory — it's how the Sun burns. Protons tunnel through their mutual electrical repulsion to fuse and release the energy that powers our solar system.

The transmission probability follows T ≈ e^(-2κa), where:
- κ = √[2m(V₀-E)]/ħ — the decay constant
- a — barrier width (exponential dependence — double the width, drastically reduce tunneling)

The visualization shows wave packets hitting barriers in real-time, with adjustable particle energy, mass, barrier height and width. Watch the probability density split between transmission and reflection.

Real-world applications include **scanning tunneling microscopes** (atomic-resolution imaging that won the 1986 Nobel Prize), **flash memory**, and **alpha decay** in radioactive materials.

Try it at [Quantum Tunneling](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/quantum-tunneling).

## 3. Hydrogen Atom Wave Function

The hydrogen atom is the only multi-electron system we can solve exactly. What we find is extraordinary: electrons don't orbit the nucleus like planets around the Sun. They exist as probability clouds — electron orbitals with shapes determined by three quantum numbers.

The visualization lets you explore all of them:
- **n (principal quantum number)** — energy level, 1, 2, 3...
- **l (azimuthal quantum number)** — orbital shape: s (spherical), p (dumbbell), d (cloverleaf), f (complex)
- **m (magnetic quantum number)** — spatial orientation, from −l to +l

The 1s orbital — hydrogen's ground state — is a sphere centered on the nucleus. The 2p orbital is a dumbbell shape with a nodal plane through the nucleus. The 3d orbitals are five different cloverleaf shapes.

This is the foundation of chemistry. Periodicity, chemical bonding, spectroscopy — all flow from these orbital shapes.

Try it at [Hydrogen Atom Wave Function](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/hydrogen-wave-function).

## 4. Infinite Square Well

The simplest quantum system — a particle trapped between two impenetrable walls — reveals the most fundamental quantum concepts.

The wave functions are standing waves: ψₙ(x) = √(2/a)·sin(nπx/a). Each state has n-1 nodes. The ground state (n=1) is most likely to be found at the center. Higher states have peaks and valleys separated by nodes.

Energy is quantized: Eₙ = n²π²ħ²/(2ma²). Note the n² dependence — this means energy levels get farther apart as n increases, unlike the harmonic oscillator's equal spacing.

The most interesting feature: **superposition states**. Combine two energy eigenstates and the probability density doesn't stay still — it oscillates. The oscillation frequency equals the energy difference divided by ħ.

This is pure quantum mechanics with no classical analog.

Try it at [Infinite Square Well](https://elysiatools.com/en/visualizations/infinite-square-well).

## Why This Matters

These four visualizations share something important: they take mathematical equations that live on blackboards and make them viscerally real. You can watch wave functions collapse against barriers. You can see electron clouds rotate in 3D. You can build a superposition and watch it evolve.

Understanding quantum mechanics isn't about memorizing formulas. It's about building intuition for a world that operates by different rules. And these tools make that possible — no signup, no software install, just open your browser.
